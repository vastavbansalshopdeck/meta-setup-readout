Meta Setup Live-Connect: First-Run Readout

Lobby → coordinator → breakout-room POC · Experiment days 7 & 8 July 2026 · (9 July excluded — day in progress)

Two days of live data confirm the core thesis: sellers will show up to an on-demand lobby, and a coordinator-routed breakout model clears them fast. The standout finding — live-connect calls run ~28% shorter than the old scheduled model (54-min median vs. 75) — directly improves the economics of the whole staffing plan. Completion sits at a healthy ~73–74%, and each POC clears ~5 sellers a day at ~66% occupancy, leaving real headroom.

1 · The headline — handle time collapsed

In the old scheduled model, a Meta setup call ran a 75-minute median (83 mean). Under live-connect, completed calls run a 54-minute median (60 mean) — roughly 28% faster. The likely driver: sellers arrive when they're actually ready and at a device, so less of the call is lost to "seller not set up yet." This is the single most important number in the readout, because it re-rates every capacity assumption we made earlier.

Why this matters for staffing

At 75-min calls, one POC in ~6 productive hours does ~4 setups. At 54-min calls, the same POC can do ~5–6. The experiment bears this out: against a time-weighted effective POC count, both days landed at ~4.8–5.8 completed setups per POC despite only ~66% occupancy — meaning there was slack to go higher.

2 · Throughput & effective hours

Counting every POC who ran a call ("active") overstates the team, because staffing ramped through the day. 7 July ran only 4 POCs early (10:30–1:00) then 10 for the rest (1:00–7:30); 8 July had people rotate in and out on leave but held ~9–10 sustained. The honest denominator is a time-weighted effective POC count — POC-hours available ÷ day length — and all per-POC productivity is calculated against that, not the raw active headcount.

Effective POC math

7 July: (4 POC × 2.5h + 10 POC × 6.5h) ÷ 9h day = 8.3 effective POCs (not 10 active).

8 July: induction/leave churn, but ~9–10 held all day → 9.5 effective POCs (not 13 active).

Why the correction matters: on the naive active count, each POC looked like ~4 setups/day. On the effective count, it's ~4.8 on 7 July and ~5.8 on 8 July — comfortably at or above the "4 ideal" productivity target we planned with, and served-per-POC of 6.6–7.8 shows genuine daily throughput. The team is more productive than the raw headcount suggested.

Effective vs. actual hours: POCs were online ~7–8 hours but on calls ~5 of those — a ~66% occupancy "effective working" ratio. The remaining third went to inter-call gaps, waiting for the coordinator to route the next seller, and (1–4 PM) lunch. That gap is reclaimable capacity: pushing occupancy from 66% → 80% on 54-min calls would lift per-POC output from ~5–6 toward ~7 setups without longer hours.

4 · Completion — and why calls fail

Completed calls are longer and fuller (54-min median) than failed ones (36-min median) — failures tend to end early when a blocker appears. The blocker pattern is remarkably consistent and is mostly seller-side and structural, not agent performance:

  • Facebook / 2FA / account-restriction issues — the dominant failure reason. "FB blocked", "2FA code not received", "account restricted", "no eligible FB account". These need a seller-side fix (new account, recovery) and a reconnect.
  • Seller not equipped — "laptop not available", "network issue", "couldn't share screen". Setup needs a desktop; mobile-only sellers can't complete.
  • Seller left mid-call — order dispatch, kids, meetings.

The 5-minute SLA is not being met

We planned for a 5-minute max lobby wait. Reality: median 6 min, and 34% of sellers waited longer than 10 minutes. On 8 July the average nearly hit 13 min as volume rose. This is the clearest operational gap — with a manual coordinator routing one-by-one, the lobby backs up at peak.

Abandonment signal: only ~9 sellers across both days entered the lobby and were never assigned, and most of those had a real reason logged (laptop unavailable, joined-and-left). We have very few clean cases to pin down the "wait-then-leave" threshold — the logging caveat (short-stay sellers at peak may be unlogged) means true abandonment is almost certainly higher than 9. We cannot yet reliably state the wait-tolerance cliff from this data.

6 · Demand shape (from lobby arrivals)

Waiting-room join times give the truest arrival signal we've had — sellers showing up on their own initiative, not pushed by a POC. 7 July shows a clean afternoon-weighted curve peaking 3–5 PM. 8 July looks front-loaded (11 AM–1 PM), but this is partly a logging artifact — many afternoon breakout calls that day have blank lobby-join times, so afternoon arrivals are undercounted.

7 · What this means for the staffing plan

The 13-agent throughput plan holds — and may be conservative
  • Handle time is better than assumed. We sized on 75-min calls / 4-per-agent. Actual is 54-min / ~5–6 completed per effective POC at only 66% occupancy. There's real slack — the team cleared 40–55 setups/day with 8.3–9.5 effective people, matching our 35–40 demand estimate with room above it.
  • Peak concurrency (12) matches our design ceiling. No need to add bodies for load; the two-shift overlap covers it.
  • The binding constraint is routing, not headcount. The 5-min SLA broke because a single human coordinator serialises assignment. Fixing that is a process/automation problem (auto-assign to first free breakout), not a hiring one.

Recommended next actions

  • Automate lobby routing to hit the 5-min SLA — replace one-by-one coordinator assignment with auto-route to the first free POC. This is the #1 fix.
  • Pre-qualify before the lobby. The top failure causes — no laptop, FB account restricted/no 2FA — are knowable before the call. A one-screen check ("Are you on a desktop? Is your Facebook login working?") would deflect ~30% of failed calls into a "fix this first" flow, lifting completion well above 74%.
  • Build a clean reconnect path. A third of failures reschedule for "tomorrow". Track and auto-surface these so they don't re-queue cold.
  • Fix logging — blank lobby-join times on completed calls and stray AM timestamps are hiding true demand and distorting occupancy. Tighten before the next read.

8 · Caveats — read the numbers with these in mind

  • Two days only. Everything here is directional. Do not rank individual POCs or lock decisions on this alone.
  • Logging is incomplete. Completed calls are reliably logged; some non-completions and short-stay lobby exits at peak are missed — so true completion rate is likely a bit lower and true abandonment higher than shown.
  • Timestamps are messy (~10-min drift, mixed AM/PM, a few 5–6 AM stray entries). Durations and spans are ±10 min; three POCs' occupancy figures are unreliable (flagged).
  • Breakout POC ≠ onboarding POC. All per-agent stats use the breakout-room POC (who ran the call). The 1–4 PM lunch window means some "free" time is actually off-shift.
  • Wait-tolerance cliff is unestablished — too few clean abandonment cases to state when sellers give up.
